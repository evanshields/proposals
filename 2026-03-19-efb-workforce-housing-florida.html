Using Essential Function Bonds to Acquire Workforce Housing in Florida

Executive Summary

Essential function bonds (EFBs) are tax‑exempt governmental purpose revenue bonds issued by public entities (typically housing authorities, housing finance authorities, or joint powers authorities) to finance facilities that perform an "essential governmental function," including affordable and workforce multifamily housing. In the contemporary workforce housing context, these bonds have been used to provide up to 100 percent financing (including acquisition, reserves, and costs of issuance) for the public acquisition of relatively new, stabilized apartment assets serving households roughly in the 60–140 percent of area median income (AMI) range, often without LIHTC and without any equity contribution from the public owner.

The core value proposition is that public ownership plus tax‑exempt debt and property tax relief free up cash flow that can be used simultaneously to lower rents to targeted income bands and to create long‑term residual value for the public owner, while private partners are compensated through fee streams and sometimes contingent cash flow participations. This report explains the legal and financial mechanics of EFBs, analyzes the Jacksonville Housing Authority's Westwood Apartments acquisition as a practical example, and then walks through how a private sponsor team could replicate the model across Florida's metro markets in partnership with housing authorities and housing finance authorities.

1. Conceptual Overview of Essential Function Bonds

1.1 Definition and Legal Character

"Essential function" or "governmental purpose" bonds are a category of tax‑exempt bonds under Section 103 of the Internal Revenue Code, issued by a state or political subdivision to finance property that the governmental issuer owns and uses for a public purpose. Unlike private‑activity multifamily housing bonds under Section 142(d) (the classic 4 percent LIHTC structure), EFBs are not subject to private‑activity volume cap so long as the financed property is a governmental facility and private use limits are respected.

In the housing context, essential function bonds typically finance multifamily projects owned directly by a housing authority, housing finance authority (HFA), city, county, or similar public instrumentality, sometimes with a long‑term ground lease to an affiliated nonprofit or project entity that is treated as part of the governmental enterprise for tax purposes. Because the issuer is the owner and the facility is deemed an essential governmental or charitable function (affordable/workforce housing), interest on the bonds is excludable from federal gross income, allowing below‑market coupon rates.

1.2 Distinction from 4% LIHTC / 142(d) Bonds and 501(c)(3) Bonds

There are three main flavors of tax‑exempt multifamily bond financing for non‑luxury housing:

  1. Qualified 142(d) multifamily housing bonds (paired with 4 percent LIHTC), where at least 20 percent of units are restricted at or below 50 percent AMI or 40 percent at or below 60 percent AMI, the borrower is typically a private partnership, and the bonds are subject to state private‑activity bond volume cap.
  2. 501(c)(3) bonds, in which a charitable nonprofit borrower owns the property and must meet either the "relief of the poor and distressed" safe harbor (Rev. Proc. 96‑32) or a "lessening the burdens of government" standard; these are also treated as qualified private‑activity bonds with their own private use constraints.
  3. Governmental / essential function bonds, where the public issuer owns the project as a governmental facility, private use is limited under general governmental bond rules, and the bonds are treated as governmental obligations under Section 103, not subject to volume cap.

In the workforce‑housing EFB model, the combination of governmental ownership, tax‑exempt financing, and property tax exemption typically replaces the LIHTC equity component; the project does not claim housing tax credits and instead relies on operating cash flow to amortize debt while providing rent discounts to targeted middle‑income households.

2. Mechanics of Workforce Housing Essential Function Bond Structures

2.1 Basic Transaction Structure

Industry practitioners such as Norris George & Ostrow describe a standard model in which a governmental issuer (city, county, housing authority, or HFA) issues unrated, non‑credit‑enhanced tax‑exempt essential function bonds to finance 100 percent of the cost of acquiring a relatively new, stabilized multifamily project. The bond proceeds cover the purchase price, fund sizable repair and operating reserves, and pay all issuance and underwriting costs, so the public owner contributes no equity.

The public entity then owns the project fee‑simple (or via a ground lease structure) from day one, and a private "project administrator" or developer affiliate enters into a long‑term services agreement to provide acquisition due diligence, asset management, property management oversight, compliance, and reporting, in exchange for an up‑front fee (often around 1–2 percent of purchase price) and ongoing asset‑management and incentive fees. Rents are set to be affordable for households generally between 60 and 120 or 140 percent of AMI, with deeper affordability at the lower end of that band, and rent increases are restricted compared to unregulated market assets.

2.2 Economics: Tax Exemption, Rent Relief, and Appreciation

Two main economic levers make the structure work:

  • Property tax relief: Projects owned by housing authorities or other exempt entities are often fully or largely exempt from local property taxation, sometimes subject to negotiated PILOT payments. Analyses by bond counsel show that, in many EFB deals, the present value of tenant rent savings for middle‑income households roughly equals or exceeds the value of the foregone property tax, and when appreciation is considered, the combined value of rent savings plus future residual equity can exceed the tax cost by four or five to one over 15–20 years.
  • Tax‑exempt debt: Because interest on EFBs is federally tax‑exempt, bonds can be issued at lower coupon rates than equivalent taxable loans, which lowers annual debt service and frees up cash flow that can be redirected into rent discounts and reserves while still maintaining adequate coverage ratios for investors.

In the common "100 percent financing" version of this structure, there is no third‑party equity; all residual value created through rent growth and appreciation (net of debt) accrues to the governmental owner, subject to any contingent performance fees owed to the project administrator. Some variants introduce subordinate public or philanthropic soft loans or use 501(c)(3) borrowers to deepen income targeting or to fit local legal frameworks, but the core economics remain: public or nonprofit ownership plus tax‑advantaged capital replaces private equity while delivering an affordability benefit.

2.3 Risk Allocation and Investor Perspective

Essential function workforce housing bonds are often sold as unrated high‑yield municipal bonds, with security limited to project revenues and project assets, and sometimes backed by additional revenues of the housing authority or issuer. Bond investors underwrite stabilised cash flows, projected rent restrictions, and property tax relief, and price the bonds accordingly, often seeking yields higher than traditional investment‑grade muni housing bonds but lower than taxable private real estate debt.

Project administrators and underwriters assume closing risk and some reputational risk: typical term sheets indicate that the administrator posts a good‑faith deposit and loses its front‑end costs if the bond issuance or property acquisition fails to close within a specified time frame, but in successful deals administrators receive substantial acquisition and asset‑management fees. The governmental issuer generally bears political and programmatic risk (e.g., scrutiny over foregone property taxes), while bondholders bear credit risk tied to project operating performance and, where applicable, broader issuer revenues.

3. Legal and Policy Context in Florida

3.1 Authorities Empowered to Issue Housing Bonds

Florida has a multilayered housing finance ecosystem that can support essential function workforce structures:

  • Local housing authorities (created under Chapter 421, Florida Statutes) are public bodies corporate and politic that may issue bonds and own projects for public housing and broader affordable housing purposes, with their projects and debentures recognized as tax‑exempt property of a housing authority.
  • County housing finance authorities (HFAs) created under Part IV of Chapter 159 are public bodies corporate and politic with express power to issue bonds for single‑family housing and qualified housing developments, to own real property, and to enter into partnerships where at least 50 percent of units benefit low‑ or very‑low‑income households.
  • The Florida Housing Finance Corporation (FHFC) is a statewide public corporation and "public instrumentality" authorized to issue revenue bonds to carry out its mission of fostering affordable housing in Florida.

These entities already run multifamily mortgage revenue bond programs using both taxable and tax‑exempt bonds to provide below‑market loans to developers, especially for LIHTC projects, and they have the statutory power to issue revenue bonds, own or ground‑lease multifamily projects, and contract with private partners.

3.2 Property Tax Exemption Framework

Property tax relief is central to EFB economics, and Florida law provides several overlapping exemptions that can be leveraged:

  • Housing authority exemption: Florida law explicitly exempts "housing projects, including all property of housing authorities used for or in connection therewith" from all state and local taxes and special assessments, with authorities authorized to make payments in lieu of taxes (PILOTs).
  • Affordable/workforce housing property exemptions: Section 196.1978 and related provisions provide 100 percent or 75 percent property tax exemptions for portions of multifamily projects used to provide affordable housing to households meeting income limits up to 120 percent AMI, subject to various ownership and regulatory conditions. Recent legislative changes expanded these exemptions to include moderate‑income and workforce properties, with specific rules for projects with 71 or more units, recorded land use restriction agreements (LURAs), and AMI bands (100 percent exemption under 80 percent AMI, 75 percent for 81–120 percent AMI).
  • Government‑owned affordable housing exemptions: Certain exemptions apply where the state or a housing finance authority owns the land and leases it long‑term for affordable housing serving households up to 120 percent AMI, again providing substantial property tax relief when conditions are met.

An essential function workforce housing structure in Florida can therefore be designed either with direct housing authority or HFA ownership (triggering the full authority exemption) or with mixed ownership and recorded FHFC/HFA regulatory agreements that qualify the project for partial or full property tax exemptions under 196.1978, in addition to the federal tax‑exempt status of the bonds.

3.3 Political and Regulatory Environment

Florida jurisdictions are actively exploring tools to expand affordable and workforce housing, including local bond programs and property tax exemptions. For example, Palm Beach County's 2022 voter‑approved housing bond program specifically targets the acquisition and construction of workforce and affordable housing, defining workforce households as between 60 and 140 percent AMI and imposing long affordability periods (50 years) for bond‑assisted rental units.

Legal guidance from Florida practitioners highlights that HFAs are empowered to issue revenue bonds and to own property through public‑private partnerships so long as statutory purposes are met and a sufficient share of units benefit low‑ and very‑low‑income households. At the same time, Florida's Attorney General and legislative materials emphasize that, as creatures of statute, HFAs and housing authorities must operate clearly within their delegated powers, so any essential function structure must be carefully aligned to statutory language and local policy priorities.

4. The Jacksonville Westwood Apartments Transaction

4.1 Deal Summary and Context

In Jacksonville, the Jacksonville Housing Authority (JHA) has begun using essential function bonds as a financing tool to acquire and rehabilitate multifamily properties as part of its strategy to address the city's housing crisis. Public reports indicate that JHA planned to acquire and renovate the 256‑unit Westwood Apartments at 1171 Lane Avenue South for approximately $140,000 per unit (about $35.8 million) plus roughly $20,000 per unit in upgrades, partnering with the Integral Group as development partner.

In early 2024, JHA completed its first major public bond sale—a general revenue bond issuance—to fund a $41 million purchase and rehab of Westwood Apartments, with the bonds secured by project rents and other Housing Authority revenues; the acquisition closed in February 2024. JHA's 2023 financial statements further note that in November 2023 the authority self‑issued $44 million in bonds to purchase Westwood, a 256‑unit market‑rate rental community, with the bond debt to be amortized from project revenues.

4.2 Affordability and Target Population

JHA has committed that once renovations are completed, approximately 82 percent of Westwood's units will be rented to households earning less than 80 percent of AMI for Jacksonville, with the remaining units rented to households earning less than 140 percent of AMI. This positioning clearly targets the "workforce" segment—teachers, nurses, service workers, and other moderate‑income households—rather than traditional deeply low‑income public housing tenants.

Local news coverage and JHA statements frame the bond issuance as an "innovative" financing mechanism that allows the agency to incur long‑term debt, secured by its A+ credit rating and project revenues, to acquire and upgrade a privately owned, stabilized asset and then operate it as affordable housing, capturing long‑term value for the authority and city.

4.3 Lessons from Westwood for Replication

Several operational lessons emerge from Westwood and similar JHA initiatives:

  • Issuer leadership and credit: JHA is explicitly using its A+ credit rating and general revenue capacity to issue essential function bonds, signaling that the agency is willing to leverage its balance sheet for workforce acquisitions, which can improve bond pricing but also increases the need for robust underwriting and governance.
  • Development partner role: JHA partnered with an experienced private developer (Integral Group) to source, underwrite, acquire, and renovate the property, with the housing authority ultimately managing the asset—mirroring the "project administrator" model where private partners provide real estate execution in exchange for fees.
  • Governance and documentation discipline: Internal JHA communications obtained by local media show that board leadership scrutinized the completeness of financial information and bond documents, underscoring that these deals attract heightened governance attention and require sophisticated financial and legal capacity on both the public and private sides.

For a private sponsor seeking to replicate the Westwood playbook elsewhere in Florida, key takeaways are that housing authorities can and will use their bonding authority and credit to acquire stabilized workforce assets where there is a credible development partner, a clear affordability plan, and strong documentation, but that political and governance scrutiny will be intense.

5. Typical Roles and Compensation for Private Partners

5.1 Project Administrator / Developer Functions

Bond counsel presentations describe a "project administrator" or "development manager" role that is analogous to a sponsor/developer in a conventional acquisition but within a public‑ownership framework. Typical responsibilities include identifying candidate properties, negotiating purchase and sale agreements, coordinating third‑party due diligence (PCA, environmental, lease audits), structuring the bond financing with underwriters and counsel, and overseeing property management and compliance over the life of the bonds.

Because the public owner often contributes no equity, the administrator typically bears significant front‑end cost risk, including nonrefundable deposits, legal and advisory fees, and organizational costs, which are reimbursed only if the bond issuance and acquisition close successfully. This creates a strong incentive for careful pre‑screening, deal selection, and transaction execution.

5.2 Fee and Incentive Structures

Industry guidance indicates that project administrators often receive:

  • An up‑front cash fee of roughly 1–2 percent (or more, depending on size) of the project purchase price at closing, funded from bond proceeds.
  • Ongoing asset‑management and administrative fees paid from project cash flow, sized to reflect the complexity of the structure and scale of the portfolio.
  • Performance‑based incentive fees tied to cash flow above specified coverage thresholds, refinancing proceeds, or sale proceeds at the end of a lockout period, often structured as a share of net residual value after full bond repayment and any required tax‑equity or public recapture.

Critically, unlike in private‑equity deals, the administrator rarely holds an equity interest in the real estate itself; instead, the public owner retains 100 percent of formal ownership and the vast majority of residual value, with private compensation tied to services and performance.

6. Designing an Essential Function Bond Strategy for Florida Workforce Housing

6.1 Strategic Fit with Florida Markets

Florida's high‑growth metros—Miami‑Dade, Broward, Palm Beach, Orlando, Tampa Bay, Southwest Florida, Jacksonville—have seen rapid rent growth and severe affordability pressures for households in the 60–140 percent AMI band, making them strong candidates for workforce EFB acquisitions. At the same time, Florida's robust property tax exemptions for affordable/workforce housing and statutory recognition of housing authorities and HFAs as tax‑exempt public instrumentalities provide a legal foundation similar to that used in California and Texas, where the essential function model has scaled.

6.2 Target Asset Profile

Based on national EFB practice and the economics of Florida property taxes and rents, attractive candidates typically share the following characteristics:

  • Vintage and quality: Relatively new (often built after 2000) Class A or B multifamily assets, already stabilized, with limited immediate capital needs but potential for value‑add upgrades.
  • Location: Suburban or infill locations near major employment centers, hospitals, schools, and transit, with strong demand from teachers, nurses, first responders, and service workers.
  • Rents: Existing rents at or slightly above levels affordable to 80–120 percent AMI households, such that modest rent reductions (enabled by tax and financing savings) still leave adequate net operating income for bond coverage.
  • Scale: Properties of at least 150–200 units, where fixed transaction costs of bond issuance and legal work can be amortized efficiently, and where Florida's property‑tax exemptions that require a minimum of 71 units for certain benefits are satisfied.

6.4 Partnership Model with Florida Issuers

A scalable strategy involves:

  1. Mapping potential issuer partners: County and city housing authorities (Miami‑Dade, Broward, Tampa, Orlando, Palm Beach, Jacksonville) and county HFAs, plus FHFC as statewide issuer where appropriate.
  2. Approaching issuers with a programmatic concept: Present a portfolio‑oriented EFB workforce acquisition program, citing Westwood and national precedent.
  3. Negotiating a master development / project‑administrator agreement: Define roles, fees, underwriting standards, affordability targets, and governance processes for each acquisition.
  4. Building a sourced pipeline: Use relationships with brokers and owners to identify suitable assets and run EFB‑specific underwriting.

8. Key Risks and Considerations

8.1 Political and Public Perception Risks

Essential function workforce deals often generate political debate over foregone property tax revenue, especially when acquiring relatively new market‑rate assets in high‑opportunity areas. Florida's legislative and media environment is attentive to perceived giveaways to developers, so narratives must emphasize rent relief for the local workforce, long‑term public ownership, and the projected ratio of public benefits to the cost of foregone taxes.

8.2 Legal and Tax Compliance Risks

To maintain tax‑exempt status under Section 103, projects must adhere to federal limits on private business use and payments. In Florida, projects must also qualify under state enabling statutes and comply with detailed requirements for property tax exemptions. Complex structures combining EFBs with LIHTC, 501(c)(3) bonds, or other subsidies require careful coordination among tax counsel, bond counsel, and regulatory agencies.

8.3 Financial and Market Risks

As high‑leverage, debt‑only structures, many essential function workforce deals are sensitive to interest‑rate conditions, rent growth assumptions, and operating cost inflation. Florida markets also face hurricane, insurance, and climate‑related cost risks that can materially affect operating expenses and reserve requirements.

8.4 Governance and Execution Capacity

Successful EFB programs require sophisticated issuer governance and capable private administrators who can manage complex bond compliance and long‑term asset stewardship. For a sponsor team, this implies investing early in top‑tier legal, financial, and compliance infrastructure.
